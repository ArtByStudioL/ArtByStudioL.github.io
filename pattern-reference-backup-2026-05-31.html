Old Country Roses — A Collector's Guide

Written by Ian McCallum  ·  Original eBay Guide

This guide will provide a seller or buyer with enough information to determine that the Old Country Roses china pattern or piece in question is genuine, correctly described, and from approximately when it was manufactured.

History & Design

King's Ransom (1921)
Old Country Roses

This range of English bone china from Royal Albert is renowned the world over, with more than 100 million pieces produced and sold. The Harold Holdcroft inspired Old Country Roses (OCR) was launched in 1962 — originally planned to be called Treasure Garden. Harold Holdcroft MSIA FRSA NRD, Royal Albert's Art Director, based his design on the 1921 pattern King's Ransom.

The design was a result of years of work. Harold Holdcroft said: "We decided to incorporate all the ingredients we knew would be acceptable — richness of colour, softness of colour, good quality and a good value look. It has lush deep red roses, warmth from the tea roses, delicacy from the bluish pink and an overall softening off of the pattern by using a shade of soft warm green. The rich gold stipple trim gives the sprays continuity and the china good value for money."

Enhanced by the elegant traditional Royal Albert Montrose shape and the attractive scalloped edges finished in 22k gold — from the twist and curl of the teacup handles to the fluted trim on the teapot lid.

English Backstamps

Where size allowed, all English produced backstamps consisted of: a floral spray (3 roses — red, yellow and pink on a green leaf background), the pattern name in red, ROYAL ALBERT or ROYAL ALBERT ®, BONE CHINA, ENGLAND or MADE IN ENGLAND, and a copyright line.

After checking literally thousands of listings, these five backstamp groups appear to be the only English manufactured range. The first four were used for tableware; the fifth for specialty and gift items.

The Five Backstamp Groups

  • Group 1 — 1962–1972, Original
  • Group 2 — 1973–1993, bottom line: Copyright Royal Albert Ltd 1962
  • Group 3 — 1973–1993, bottom line: Copyright 1962 Royal Albert Ltd
  • Group 4 — 1993–2002, top line: Royal Albert © Registered; bottom: Copyright 1962 Royal Albert Ltd
  • Group 5 — 1993–2002, Made in England (specialty items)

Ian McCallum's view: the original 1962–1972 pieces should be considered most valuable. Harold Holdcroft retired in late 1972 and his attention to detail ensured high quality standards throughout this period.

Factory Seconds

Whenever a product was too good to discard but not good enough to meet first quality standard, the backstamp was scratched or marked, then sold at a discount — usually 50% or more. Common flaws: mis-alignment of graphic transfer, size variation, glazing or gilding flaw, minor impurity or firing pitting, colour variation.

Do not believe a claim that a second is as good as a first. The item became a second for a specific reason. If the seller can't tell you what it is, it just means they can't find it — not that it's not there.

Note: gilders often put a test mark on the back — this does not indicate a flaw.

Asian Production

Production in Asia started as early as 1998. By December 20, 2002, most Royal Albert production including Old Country Roses was transferred to Indonesia, and some to China. Royal Doulton was taken over by Waterford Wedgwood in January 2005 — the sixth owner in OCR's 44-year existence.

The Four Most Common Buying Mistakes

  1. Buying Indonesian or Chinese OCR thinking it is English
  2. Buying 1962 backstamps believing it is the year of manufacture
  3. Buying factory seconds thinking they are first quality
  4. Not checking descriptions for accurate condition of product

Whenever you do not see the word ENGLAND on a backstamp, the item is Asian — most likely Indonesian. Many sellers do not mention the origin of manufacture in their description, so always check the backstamp carefully.
